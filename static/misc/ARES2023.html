  1. ‌INTRODUCTION

    Threat intelligence sharing is a promising solution to enhance knowledge and situational awareness of the rapidly growing num- ber of emerging cyber threats. Accordingly, there are a variety of platforms on the security solutions market that enable the efficient and targeted exchange of threat intelligence across organisations. Unfortunately, very little is known so far about the dissemination and use of these platforms from the end-user perspective. To address this issue, we conducted an exploratory study on the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms in Germany, Austria and Switzerland. For this purpose, we surveyed 69 security and IT experts from large companies, federal authorities and public universities in autumn 2022. Our findings show, among other things, a growing interest in threat intelligence sharing platforms and their value to information security processes.

    The complexity, heterogeneity, and global distribution of modern information systems, combined with the increasing number of so- phisticated cyber attacks, pose a serious threat to the security of IT systems [22, 31]. Organisations often do not have sufficient resources to protect themselves in time against emerging cyber threats. To counteract this, threat intelligence sharing is a promis- ing solution to develop adequate knowledge and better situational awareness of the rapidly growing number of new cyber threats [36]. Threat intelligence sharing includes the systematic collection, analysis, evaluation and sharing of threat intelligence within and across organisations [15, 35]. It enables faster, more targeted and effi- cient detection and combating of security incidents. It is an essential aid to improve the security situation in organisations [21] and is a prerequisite for compliance with standards (e.g. ISO27002:2022) [23]. According to [13, 47] threat intelligence can be divided into the following four types: strategic, tactical, operational, and technical. Strategic cyber threat intelligence provides high-level information on changing organizational security risks. Tactical cyber threat intelligence outlines attackers’ methodologies, while operational cyber threat intelligence describes details of specific attacks against an organization. Technical cyber threat intelligence focuses on indicators of compromise (e.g. malicious IP addresses or hashes of malware files). However, organisations often do not have suffi- cient resources to adequately implement sharing of threat intelli- gence [19]. Accordingly, it requires greater automation of subtasks (i.e., collection, pre-processing, analysis, dissemination and evalua-

    tion [18, 35]) of the threat intelligence cycle [15].

    To address this issue, various threat intelligence sharing plat- forms emerged on the market during the last 15 years [37]. These are software solutions that facilitate collection of threat data from multiple sources, their aggregation and joint analysis, as well as the exchange and assessment of the resulting threat intelligence [4, 15, 35]. We define threat intelligence sharing platforms as inter- organizational systems that enable the provision, management, and exchange of threat intelligence among multiple organizations [35, 41]. Globally known platforms include, for example, Anomali Threat- Stream, Crowdstrike Falcon X Intelligence, EclecticIQ, IBM X-Force Exchange, Malware Information Sharing Platform (MISP), Open Threat Exchange (OTX), ThreatConnect, ThreatQ, and Trellix Threat Intelligence Exchange.


    While most academic research focuses on platforms [37] and their functionalities [4, 35], there is a lack of comprehensive em- pirical research on their distribution and use of these platforms from an end-user perspective. To address this gap we focus on the following three research questions: (RQ1) How widespread are threat intelligence sharing platforms in Germany, Austria and Switzerland?, (RQ2) How are threat intelligence sharing platforms used in organisa- tions? and (RQ3) What are threat intelligence sharing platforms used for in organisations?

    To answer these research questions, we conducted an exploratory study in Germany, Austria, and Switzerland in autumn 2022 with 69 security experts from large companies, federal authorities, and public universities. We conducted an online survey to analyse how widespread the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms is and how exactly and for what purpose they are used in organisations. Our investigations resulted in eight key findings, that show a grow- ing interest in threat intelligence sharing platforms and their value to information security processes.

    The remainder of the paper is structured as follows: In Section 2, we discuss related work of empirical research in the field of threat intelligence sharing. In Section 3, we outline how the exploratory study was prepared and conducted. In Section 4, we present and discuss the key findings of our survey and the limitations of the research at hand. Section 5 concludes our work and provides an outlook on future work.


  2. ‌RELATED WORK‌

    While research initially tended to focus on the opportunities, require- ments and challenges [1, 41, 43, 45, 48, 49, 51], legal and regulatory

    aspects [12, 33, 40, 45], standardisation [3, 14, 24, 30, 39, 45], and im-

    plementation [11, 32, 34, 37, 41, 45, 46, 48] of threat intelligence shar- ing platforms, there has recently been a surge in empirical studies exploring certain organisational aspects [4, 6–8, 16, 17, 20, 28, 35, 37– 39, 44–46, 52–54] of threat intelligence sharing. These studies con- centrate on the integration of threat intelligence platforms into information security management [20], the perceived benefits and barriers of threat intelligence sharing [54], and data quality of shared threat intelligence [8, 38, 53].

    In addition, various works deal with criteria and characteristics for the evaluation and selection of threat intelligence sharing plat- forms [4, 6, 17, 35]. In this context, a model for determining the maturity of threat intelligence platforms [44] and a capability ma- turity model using the level of cyber threat intelligence integration as a proxy for security operations center service maturity were developed [39].

    While most of the aforementioned publications focus on the provider side of threat intelligence sharing platforms, a few pub- lications also examine the end-user perspective. This involves in- vestigating how users perceive the graphical user interfaces [46] and how satisfied users are with threat intelligence sharing plat- forms [16, 52]. There are some global non-academic studies [9, 10, 29] that investigate threat intelligence sharing but do not focus on platforms or tools for threat intelligence sharing.

    As already mentioned above, most studies focus on the provider side of threat intelligence sharing platforms and only a few on the end-user perspective. Our exploratory study fills this gap by

    examining the prevalence and use of threat intelligence sharing platforms in detail.

  3. ‌RESEARCH METHODOLOGY‌

    To investigate the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms from an end-user perspective, we conducted an empirical study on the methodologies proposed by [26] and [27].

    To specify the aim of our study and research questions, we first made as concrete claims as possible about the use of threat intel- ligence sharing platforms. We base our claims on our previous research and related work in the field of cyber threat intelligence sharing (see Section 2). As part of our research over the last five years, we have comprehensively examined more than fifty threat intelligence sharing platforms on the market. In this context, we conducted numerous expert interviews, primarily with providers of the platforms, but also with some users. In addition, we were guided by works that present criteria for the evaluation of threat in- telligence sharing platforms [4, 17, 35] and have conducted further market studies [37]. Beside this more provider-oriented perspective, we have incorporated the limited empirical evidence on the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms into our claims. In this context, we primarily looked at the studies of the SANS [9, 10] and Ponemon Institutes [29].

    Based on these information sources, we derived the following claims about the dissemination of threat intelligence sharing plat- forms and their use:

    C1 The use of threat intelligence sharing platforms has increased over the last four years.

    C2 Threat intelligence sharing platforms are more widespread in companies than in authorities and universities.

    C3 The most common reasons why threat intelligence sharing platforms are not used are limited resources and data privacy and compliance concerns.

    C4 Despite the growing range of threat intelligence sharing platforms, there is a concentration on single, dominant plat- forms.

    C5 The simultaneous use of several threat intelligence sharing platforms is rather rare.

    C6 Threat intelligence sharing platforms are preferred for collec- tion, pre-processing and analysis rather than dissemination and evaluation of threat intelligence.

    C7 Organisations most frequently use threat intelligence shar- ing platforms to support incident management.

    C8 Organisations tend to share threat intelligence via platforms only internally and not externally.

    To explore these eight claims, we chose an online survey for data collection [5]. Therefore, we designed a three-part questionnaire with a total of 26 questions [50].

    The first part of the questionnaire (questions Q1 to Q10) serves to characterise the respondents and their organisations. This part provides questions about the size and type of organisation, the geographical distribution, and the role of the respondents within the organisation.

    The second part contains questions (cf. Q11 AND ((Q12 to Q15) OR (Q16 to Q20)) on the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms. It also addresses the cases where organisations are still

  4. ‌KEY FINDINGS‌

    In this section, we present the key findings of our study. We discuss the results obtained from examining the eight claims (see Section 4.1 to 4.8) and address the limitations associated with our work (see Section 4.9).

    1. ‌Platforms are widespread and their use will increase‌

      C1 states that the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms has increased over the last four years. This claim is based on study results, such as the SANS reports from 2021 [9] and 2022 [10]. These reports show that in recent years the use of such platforms has increased worldwide. In order to be able to make a statement about this, we asked the survey participants whether and from when

      they use threat intelligence sharing platforms. Figure 1 summarises our findings. More than half of the organisations surveyed (53.6%) use threat intelligence sharing platforms. Furthermore, Figure 1 shows that the use has increased almost continuously over the last four years. These two findings speak both to our claim (C1) and to the fact that threat intelligence sharing platforms have arrived in practice. We also identified which of the organisations surveyed plan to use a platform in the coming years. As depicted in Figure 1 (cf. the two right bars), 21.7% of the organisations answered in the affirmative. About 7% even intend to use a platform in the next two years. Accordingly, the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms is expected to continue to grow significantly over the next five years.

    2. ‌Platforms are more widespread in companies than in authorities and universities

      If we look at the answers on the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms in detail, there are clear differences between companies, authorities and universities. In C2 we claimed that use is more widespread in companies. As depicted in Figure 2, the dissemination of threat intelligence sharing platforms is highest in companies. Over 84% of the companies surveyed stated that they use platforms. Among authorities, it is just under half and among universities only one in three. Figure 2 also shows that quite a number of authorities (20.7%) and universities (33.3%) indicated that they plan to use threat intelligence sharing platforms in the coming years. Consequently,

      it can be assumed that the current large gap to the companies will narrow substantially in the near future.


    3. ‌The main reason for not using a platform is a lack of resources

      Among the organisations surveyed, there were also some that do not want to use threat intelligence sharing platforms either now or in the future (cf. Figure 2). We were interested in understanding the reasons for not using such platforms. Based on several studies [2, 25, 36, 54] we suspected a lack of resources as well as data privacy and compliance concerns (cf. C3). Figure 3 gives an overview of the reasons given by the respective survey participants for not using threat intelligence sharing platforms. Among the first three reasons mentioned are two resource problems, namely a lack of staff to operate and use the platform and insufficient financial resources. Surprisingly, data protection and compliance was only cited by two participants as a reason for non-use.


    4. ‌Malware Information Sharing Platform is the dominant platform

      There are hardly any empirical findings on market shares or fre- quencies of use of individual threat intelligence sharing platforms. We assumed that, similar to other domains, there is a concentra- tion on a few single, dominant platforms (cf. C4). To this end, we asked all 37 organisations in our sample that use threat intelligence sharing platforms which platforms they use. As documented in Figure 4, the Malware Information Sharing Platform (MISP) is the dominant platform. This result is not surprising, as MISP is one of the first and globally known platforms. Moreover, it is one of the few European platforms and thus very widespread in Europe. However, the clarity of the result is nevertheless surprising, but speaks even more strongly in favor of our assumption. A closer look at these results shows that the first two places are taken by MISP and Open Threat Exchange, two so called open-source or free-to-use platforms. They are followed by commercial platforms, e.g., Crowdstrike Falcon X Intelligence and ThreatQ. Freely accessi- ble platforms are thus more widespread among the organisations surveyed than commercial ones. This is surprising and contradicts, for example, the SANS report from 2022 [10], which shows that in recent years more commercial platforms have been used worldwide than freely accessible ones.

    5. ‌Simultaneous use of multiple platforms is popular

      Regarding the manner in which threat tntelligence sharing plat- forms are used, we assumed that the simultaneous use of platforms is rather rare (cf. C5). However, our research results shown in Fig- ure 5 do not support this assumption. Instead, we found that almost half (43.2%) of the organisations using threat intelligence shar- ing platforms use multiple platforms at the same time. In these cases, two platforms are usually used in parallel. One organisation reported using three platforms and two organisations use four plat- forms simultaneously. A more detailed analysis of the responses revealed that the combination of freely available and commercial platforms is very popular.

    6. ‌Platforms are used extensively not only for the collection, pre-processing and analysis but also for the dissemination of threat intelligence

      Five groups of functions offered by threat intelligence sharing plat- forms can be distinguished: functions for collection, pre-processing, analysis, dissemination, and evaluation of threat intelligence [35]. Based on the related work we expected, that threat intelligence sharing platforms are preferred for collection, pre-processing and analysis rather than dissemination and evaluation of threat intelli- gence (cf.C6). However, as shown in Figure 6, our analysis yielded different results. The bar chart documents which platform func- tions are used more regularly and which are used less regularly. The collection, pre-processing, analysis and dissemination functions tend to be used regularly, while the evaluation functions are used only irregularly or not at all.


      ‌Figure 7: Areas of application of the platforms


      Moreover, Figure 6 shows differences in the frequency of use of the functions. The light-coloured parts of the bars document the daily and thus the most frequent use of the respective functions. The results of the survey show that organisations use the collection (59.5%) and pre-processing (45.9%) functions considerably more often compared to all other functions. The analysis and dissemina- tion functions are reported to be used by only about one-third of organisations on a daily basis.

    7. ‌Three main areas of application of the platforms

      In various studies [9, 42], incident management is mentioned as the most common application area for threat intelligence sharing plat- forms. As this was consistent with our experience, we also assumed that the platforms would be used most often to support incident management (cf. C7). Accordingly, we asked which information security processes or tasks are supported by threat intelligence sharing platforms in organisations. As depicted in Figure 7 sur- vey participants most frequently mentioned incident management, threat hunting and malware analysis. These results confirm our assumption.

    8. ‌Majority use platforms for sharing threat intelligence with externals‌

      Critics of the concept of threat intelligence sharing claim that or- ganisations are reluctant to share their threat intelligence with external parties and therefore tend to share threat data across plat- forms only internally and not externally (cf. C8). As depicted in Figure 8, almost 41% of the survey participants answered that they use the platforms to share threat and security information with

      both internal and external organisations or partners. Another 13.5% use threat intelligence sharing platforms exclusively for sharing TI outside the organisation. This is an argument against C8. Our findings show that more than half of the companies covered by our study use threat intelligence sharing platforms to share TI with external partners.

    9. ‌Limitations‌

      The research at hand might be limited by a (i) selection bias of par- ticipants, (ii) selection bias of claims and (iii) low sample size. In order to counteract (i), we contacted companies of the most im- portant stock indices, federal authorities and public universities in Germany, Austria and Switzerland. However, this does not com- pletely invalidate (i), as it is not a random sample. Our findings are primarily exploratory in nature and should not be understood as generally valid statements. Therefore, the question will soon no longer be whether to use them, but how to use them even more effec- tively and efficiently. Organisations that do not use these platforms most often justify this with a lack of resources. In organisations that use threat intelligence sharing platforms, free-to-use or open- source platforms are the most common. The Malware Information Sharing Platform (MISP) is the most widely used platform. The platforms are regularly used by organisations not only for the col- lection, pre-processing and analysis, but also for the dissemination of threat intelligence. In most organisations, threat intelligence is shared across organisation boundaries.

      This study provides initial valuable insights. This is a good start- ing point for further studies. Our future work will focus on compara- ble studies with larger, ideally randomly selected samples. Regular repetition would allow us to identify changes and trends in the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms. Moreover, an interna- tional expansion of the study is also planned, which will enable comparative country- and region-specific statements on the use of the platforms.

  5. ‌CONCLUSION & OUTLOOK‌

In this paper we provide an exploratory analysis of the use of threat intelligence sharing platforms in Germany, Austria and Switzerland. We surveyed 69 security and IT experts in autumn 2022. In doing so, detailed insights on the dissemination of threat intelligence sharing platforms in large companies, federal authorities and public universities were identified. We also analysed how exactly and for what purpose threat intelligence sharing platforms are used in the organisations.

The findings show that threat intelligence sharing platforms are well established in practice and are already used by more than half of the organizations covered by our study. While such platforms are most common in companies, agencies and universities are planning
